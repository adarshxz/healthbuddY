# Diabetes Mellitus

## Overview
Diabetes is a chronic condition characterized by high blood glucose (sugar) levels. It occurs when the pancreas does not produce enough insulin (Type 1) or when the body cannot effectively use the insulin it produces (Type 2). Consistent management is key to preventing complications.

## Key Complications
- **Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar):** Blood glucose < 70 mg/dL. Can occur suddenly due to skipping meals, excessive medication, or unexpected physical activity.
- **Hyperglycemia (High Blood Sugar):** Consistently elevated blood glucose levels. If left untreated, it can progress to Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) or Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS), which are life-threatening.

## Home Care & Management
- **Hypoglycemia Treatment (The 15-15 Rule):**
  1. Consume 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates (e.g., 4 ounces of fruit juice, 3-4 glucose tablets, or 1 tablespoon of sugar/honey).
  2. Wait 15 minutes and check blood glucose.
  3. If still under 70 mg/dL, repeat the steps.
  4. Once normal, eat a small snack or meal containing complex carbs and protein to stabilize blood sugar.
- **Hyperglycemia Management:** Stay hydrated by drinking water, monitor blood sugar levels closely, take prescribed insulin or oral medications, and check for ketones if blood sugar is consistently above 240 mg/dL (especially in Type 1 diabetes).
- **Lifestyle:** Maintain a balanced diet monitoring carbohydrate intake, engage in regular exercise (check blood sugar before exercising), and inspect feet daily for cuts, blisters, or signs of infection.

## Red Flags & Emergency
Seek immediate emergency medical attention if you experience:
- Confusion, extreme drowsiness, irritability, or loss of consciousness (signs of severe hypoglycemia).
- Deep, rapid breathing, fruit-scented breath, nausea/vomiting, and abdominal pain (signs of Diabetic Ketoacidosis).
- High blood sugar levels that do not respond to insulin or medication, accompanied by confusion, dry mouth, and high fever.
- Cuts or wounds on the feet that show signs of infection (pus, redness, spreading warmth, or foul odor) and do not heal.
